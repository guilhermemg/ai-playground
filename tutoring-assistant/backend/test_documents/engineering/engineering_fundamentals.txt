Engineering Fundamentals

1. MECHANICS OF MATERIALS

1.1 Stress and Strain
Stress (σ) is the internal force per unit area within a material: σ = F/A, measured in Pascals (Pa) or N/m². Strain (ε) is the deformation relative to the original dimension: ε = ΔL/L (dimensionless). Stress and strain are related by material properties.

1.2 Hooke's Law
In the elastic (linear) region, stress is proportional to strain: σ = Eε, where E is Young's modulus (modulus of elasticity). This relationship holds until the proportional limit is reached. Beyond the elastic limit, the material undergoes permanent (plastic) deformation.

1.3 Types of Stress
- Tensile stress: Force pulling the material apart
- Compressive stress: Force pushing the material together
- Shear stress (τ): Force acting parallel to a surface, τ = F/A
- Bending stress: Combination of tensile and compressive stress due to bending moment
- Torsional stress: Shear stress due to twisting

1.4 Material Properties
- Yield strength: Stress at which plastic deformation begins
- Ultimate tensile strength (UTS): Maximum stress a material can withstand
- Ductility: Ability to deform plastically before fracture
- Brittleness: Tendency to fracture without significant plastic deformation
- Hardness: Resistance to surface indentation
- Fatigue strength: Resistance to failure under cyclic loading

2. THERMODYNAMICS

2.1 Laws of Thermodynamics
- Zeroth Law: If A is in thermal equilibrium with B, and B with C, then A is in thermal equilibrium with C. This establishes the concept of temperature.
- First Law: Energy is conserved. ΔU = Q - W, where U is internal energy, Q is heat added, W is work done by the system.
- Second Law: Entropy of an isolated system never decreases. Heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold. No heat engine can be 100% efficient.
- Third Law: As temperature approaches absolute zero, the entropy of a perfect crystal approaches zero.

2.2 Heat Transfer
Three modes of heat transfer:
- Conduction: Transfer through direct molecular contact. Q/t = kA(ΔT/L), where k is thermal conductivity.
- Convection: Transfer by fluid motion. Q/t = hA(ΔT), where h is the convection coefficient.
- Radiation: Transfer by electromagnetic waves. Q/t = εσA(T⁴), where ε is emissivity and σ is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant.

2.3 Thermodynamic Cycles
- Carnot cycle: Ideal reversible cycle with maximum efficiency η = 1 - T_cold/T_hot
- Otto cycle: Idealized gasoline engine cycle
- Diesel cycle: Idealized diesel engine cycle
- Rankine cycle: Idealized steam power plant cycle
- Refrigeration cycle: Reversed heat engine used for cooling

3. CONTROL SYSTEMS

3.1 Open-Loop vs Closed-Loop Systems
An open-loop system has no feedback — the output does not influence the input. A closed-loop (feedback) system uses output measurement to adjust the input, improving accuracy and stability.

3.2 Feedback Control
Negative feedback subtracts the output signal from the reference input, reducing error. The error signal e(t) = r(t) - y(t), where r(t) is the reference and y(t) is the output. Negative feedback improves stability, reduces sensitivity to disturbances, and improves transient response.

Positive feedback adds the output to the input, amplifying the signal. It is generally destabilizing and used in specific applications (oscillators, Schmitt triggers).

3.3 PID Controller
The PID (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) controller is the most widely used feedback controller:
- Proportional (P): Output proportional to current error. Reduces error but may not eliminate steady-state error.
- Integral (I): Output proportional to accumulated past error. Eliminates steady-state error but may cause overshoot.
- Derivative (D): Output proportional to rate of change of error. Reduces overshoot and improves stability.
- Combined: u(t) = Kp·e(t) + Ki·∫e(t)dt + Kd·de(t)/dt

3.4 Stability Analysis
- BIBO stability: A system is stable if bounded inputs produce bounded outputs
- Root locus: Plot of closed-loop poles as gain varies
- Bode plot: Frequency response analysis (magnitude and phase vs frequency)
- Nyquist criterion: Determines stability from the open-loop frequency response

4. FLUID MECHANICS

4.1 Properties of Fluids
- Density (ρ): Mass per unit volume, ρ = m/V
- Viscosity (μ): Resistance to flow. Newton's law of viscosity: τ = μ(du/dy)
- Pressure: Force per unit area, P = F/A. In a static fluid, pressure increases with depth: P = P₀ + ρgh

4.2 Bernoulli's Equation
For steady, incompressible, inviscid flow along a streamline:
P + (1/2)ρv² + ρgh = constant
This relates pressure, velocity, and elevation. Applications include flow measurement (Venturi meter, Pitot tube) and explaining lift on airplane wings.

4.3 Reynolds Number
Re = ρvD/μ, a dimensionless number that predicts flow regime:
- Re < 2300: Laminar flow (smooth, orderly)
- Re > 4000: Turbulent flow (chaotic, with eddies)
- 2300 < Re < 4000: Transitional flow
